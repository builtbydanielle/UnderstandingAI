UNDERSTANDING AI FUNDAMENTALS

HOW AI WORKS

Model

A trained mathematical system that maps input to output.

Training

Feeding large amounts of data so the model learns patterns.

Parameters

Internal numbers storing what the model learned.

Inference

Using the trained model to produce an answer.

Tokens

Small chunks of text processed by language models.

Context Window

How much information the model can consider at once.

What AI Is

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is software that learns patterns from data and uses those patterns to make predictions, generate content, or classify information.

Think of AI as an extremely advanced pattern engine—not a thinking being.

What AI Does

  • Predict what is statistically likely
  • Imitate patterns it has seen before
  • Generate content even when wrong

What AI Does NOT Do

  • Think
  • Understand meaning
  • Feel emotion
  • Have intent or awareness
Key Technical Concepts

Neural Networks

Systems inspired by the human brain, made of interconnected nodes that process information.

Algorithms

Step-by-step procedures for solving problems within AI systems.

Datasets

Large collections of data used to train AI models. Quality impacts performance.

Hallucinations

When AI generates false information that sounds plausible but isn't true.

Bias

AI can reflect and amplify biases from training data.

AGI (Artificial General Intelligence)

Hypothetical AI with human-level intelligence across all domains. Can learn any intellectual task a human can. Does not exist yet. All current AI is "Narrow AI" - designed for specific tasks only.

ASI (Artificial Superintelligence)

Theoretical AI that surpasses human intelligence in all areas. Beyond AGI. Purely speculative - not close to existing. Subject of much debate about risks and benefits.

Fine-tuning vs RAG

Fine-tuning: Adjusting a model with specific data by retraining it.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): Giving the model external knowledge to reference in real-time. The AI searches a database or documents, retrieves relevant information, then generates a response using that context. Like giving AI a library to consult before answering.

LLM (Large Language Model)

AI models trained on massive amounts of text data. Can generate human-like text, answer questions, write code. Examples: GPT-4, Claude, Llama. "Large" refers to billions of parameters.

NLP (Natural Language Processing)

The field of AI focused on understanding and generating human language. Powers chatbots, translation, sentiment analysis, and text summarization.

CV (Computer Vision)

AI that can "see" and interpret images and video. Used for facial recognition, medical imaging, autonomous vehicles, and object detection.

Few-shot & Zero-shot Learning

Few-shot: AI performs a task after seeing just a few examples. Zero-shot: AI performs a task it's never been explicitly trained on. This is how you use AI daily—give it 2-3 examples and it understands the pattern.

Learning Methods

Supervised Learning

AI learns from labeled examples (input-output pairs).

Example: Email spam detection

Unsupervised Learning

AI finds patterns in data without labeled examples.

Example: Customer segmentation

Reinforcement Learning

AI learns through trial and error, receiving rewards and penalties.

Example: Game-playing AI, robotics

MAIN TYPES OF AI

Generative AI

What it is: AI that creates new content from scratch.

  • Generates text, images, audio, video, and code
  • Examples: ChatGPT, DALL-E, Midjourney, Claude
  • Uses patterns learned from training data to create original outputs
Machine Learning (ML)

What it is: Systems that learn patterns from data to make predictions.

  • Learns patterns from data
  • Improves with more examples
  • Outputs probabilities, not certainties
  • Powers recommendation engines, fraud detection
Deep Learning

What it is: A type of ML using neural networks with many layers.

  • Used for images, speech, and language
  • Requires large amounts of data
  • Powers facial recognition, voice assistants
  • Foundation for most modern AI breakthroughs
Predictive/Discriminative AI

What it is: AI that classifies data or predicts outcomes.

  • Classifies or predicts outcomes
  • Used for fraud detection, credit scoring
  • Makes recommendations based on patterns
  • Doesn't create new content, analyzes existing data
Narrow AI

What it is: AI designed for specific tasks only.

  • Built for specific tasks only
  • All AI in use today is Narrow AI
  • Cannot transfer skills to other domains
  • Examples: Chess AI, spam filters, voice recognition
Multimodal AI

What it is: AI that works across multiple types of data.

  • Works across text, image, audio, video
  • Can understand and generate multiple formats
  • Examples: GPT-4, Gemini, Claude
  • Can analyze an image and describe it in text
Agentic AI

What it is: AI that can take actions autonomously to achieve goals, not just respond to prompts.

  • Can use tools (browse web, run code, access databases)
  • Makes decisions about which actions to take
  • Works through multi-step tasks without constant human direction
  • Examples: AI that can research a topic, book appointments, or debug code end-to-end
  • Often called "AI agents" - think of them as AI assistants with agency

Open-Source vs Closed-Source Models

Closed-Source (Proprietary)

What it is: Models owned and controlled by companies. You access them through APIs or web interfaces.

Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Midjourney

Pros: Most powerful, constantly updated, easy to use, no setup required

Cons: Costs money, data goes to company servers, limited customization, vendor lock-in

Open-Source

What it is: Models with publicly available code and weights. You can download and run them yourself.

Examples: Llama, Mistral, Stable Diffusion, Whisper

Pros: Free to use, complete control, data stays private, full customization, run locally

Cons: Requires technical setup, needs powerful hardware, often less capable than closed-source, you maintain it

Which Should You Use?

Choose Closed-Source if: You want the best performance, need it to work immediately, don't have technical resources, are okay with subscription costs

Choose Open-Source if: You handle sensitive data, need complete control, have technical expertise, want to avoid vendor lock-in, need to customize extensively

Hybrid approach: Many companies use closed-source for general tasks and open-source for sensitive/specialized work.

Running AI Locally on Your Computer

What Does "Running Locally" Mean?

Instead of sending your prompts to a company's servers (like ChatGPT or Claude), you download an AI model and run it entirely on your own computer. Your data never leaves your machine.

Why Run AI Locally?

  • Privacy: Sensitive data stays on your computer
  • Cost: Free after initial setup, no monthly subscriptions
  • Control: Full customization, no usage limits
  • Offline access: Works without internet
  • No censorship: No content restrictions or filters

The Easiest Way: Ollama

What it does: One-click download and run of popular models (Llama, Mistral, etc.)

Works on: Mac, Windows, Linux

Free: Completely open-source

Hardware Requirements

  • 16GB RAM
  • 10-20GB storage per model
  • Modern CPU (any from last 5 years)

Recommended (medium models):

  • 32GB+ RAM
  • Graphics card with 8GB+ VRAM

High-end (large models):

  • 64GB+ RAM
  • High-end GPU (RTX 4090, etc.)

Step 2: Install like any normal app

Step 3: Open terminal and type: ollama run llama3

When NOT to Run Locally

  • You need the absolute best quality (cloud models like GPT-4 are still better)
  • You don't have powerful hardware
  • You want zero setup - just type and go
  • You need features like web browsing, image generation (local models have limitations)
  • Your team needs to share the same AI system

When Running Locally Makes Sense

  • You work with confidential data (legal, medical, financial)
  • You want to avoid monthly AI subscription costs
  • You need offline access
  • You're developing AI applications and need full control
  • You want to experiment with fine-tuning models

Understanding Temperature & Creativity Settings

What is Temperature?

A setting that controls how creative or conservative AI responses are. Scale usually 0 to 1 (or 0 to 2).

Low temperature (0-0.3): More predictable, factual, consistent. Best for data analysis, coding, technical writing.

High temperature (0.7-1.0): More creative, varied, unpredictable. Best for brainstorming, creative writing, marketing copy.

When to Adjust Temperature

Use LOW for: Math problems, code debugging, factual summaries, legal documents, technical documentation

Use HIGH for: Creative stories, marketing taglines, brainstorming ideas, poetry, exploring possibilities

Default (0.5-0.7): Works for most everyday tasks

Token Limits & Context Windows

What Are Tokens?

Tokens are chunks of text that AI processes. Roughly: 1 token = 4 characters or 0.75 words.

Example: "Hello world" = 2 tokens. "Artificial Intelligence" = 4 tokens.

Why Token Limits Matter

Every AI model has a maximum number of tokens it can process at once (input + output combined). This is called the "context window."

Example limits:

  • GPT-3.5: ~4,000 tokens (~3,000 words)
  • GPT-4: ~8,000-128,000 tokens depending on version
  • Claude: ~200,000 tokens (~150,000 words)

What Happens When You Hit the Limit

The AI either:

  • Cuts off mid-response (output stops abruptly)
  • Forgets earlier parts of the conversation
  • Refuses to process your request

Solution: Break large tasks into smaller chunks, summarize previous context, or use a model with a larger context window.

Real Prompt Examples: Before & After

VAGUE PROMPT (Gets Poor Results)

"Write something about marketing."

Why it fails: No context, no constraints, no direction. AI has to guess what you want.

SPECIFIC PROMPT (Gets Great Results)

"Write a 3-paragraph email to marketing managers explaining how AI can automate social media scheduling. Use a professional but friendly tone. Include one specific example of time saved."

Why it works: Clear purpose, defined audience, specific format, tone specified, concrete requirement included.

VAGUE PROMPT (Gets Poor Results)

"Analyze this data."

Why it fails: What kind of analysis? What should it focus on? What format?

SPECIFIC PROMPT (Gets Great Results)

"Analyze this sales data and identify the top 3 trends. Focus on month-over-month growth patterns. Present findings as bullet points with specific percentages. Flag any anomalies that need investigation."

Why it works: Specifies analysis type, focus area, output format, and what to prioritize.

VAGUE PROMPT (Gets Poor Results)

"Help me write code."

Why it fails: What language? What should it do? What's the context?

SPECIFIC PROMPT (Gets Great Results)

"Write a Python function that takes a list of email addresses and returns only the valid ones. Use regex for validation. Include comments explaining each step. Handle common edge cases like missing @ symbols."

Why it works: Language specified, clear input/output, implementation details, documentation requirements, edge cases considered.

Step-by-Step: Your First AI Conversation

STEP 1: Start with a Clear Goal

Before you type anything, ask yourself: "What specific outcome do I want?"

Example goal: "I need a professional email declining a meeting request."

STEP 2: Provide Context

Give the AI background information it needs to help you effectively.

"I'm a marketing manager. A vendor wants to meet about a product we're not interested in. I want to decline politely but firmly, keeping the door open for future opportunities."

STEP 3: Specify Format and Constraints

Tell the AI exactly how you want the output structured.

"Write this as a 2-3 paragraph email. Professional tone. Keep it under 150 words."

STEP 4: Review and Refine

The first output is rarely perfect. Tell the AI what to adjust.

"Make it warmer" or "Remove the second paragraph" or "Change the opening line to be more direct."

STEP 5: Always Verify

Never use AI output without reviewing it. Check for accuracy, tone, and appropriateness. Make it yours.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Treating AI Like a Search Engine

AI generates answers, it doesn't retrieve facts. Always verify factual claims, especially dates, statistics, and technical details.

Accepting the First Response

AI can do better with guidance. If the output isn't quite right, ask for adjustments rather than starting over.

Being Too Polite

You don't need to say "please" and "thank you." Be direct and clear. AI doesn't have feelings.

Expecting Mind Reading

AI doesn't know your context unless you provide it. The more specific you are, the better the results.

Using It for Everything

Some tasks are faster to do yourself. AI is best for complex, time-consuming, or creative work.

Trusting Without Verifying

AI makes mistakes confidently. Always fact-check important information before using it.

AI Limitations

Knowledge Cutoffs

Models only know information up to their training date.

No True Reasoning

AI uses pattern matching, not logical reasoning.

Math & Logic Struggles

Complex calculations can produce errors. Always verify.

Context Limits

Can only process a limited amount of text at once.

Hallucinations

AI can confidently generate false information.

No Real-Time Data

Without search tools, AI doesn't know current events.

ETHICS & CONSIDERATIONS

Privacy Concerns

Be cautious about sharing sensitive personal information. Assume data you input may be stored and used for training. Never share passwords, financial details, or confidential business information.

Copyright & Ownership

Questions remain about who owns AI-generated content. Many AI-generated outputs cannot be copyrighted. Understand the legal landscape and platform terms before commercial use.

Job Displacement

AI is automating tasks previously done by humans. While some jobs may be eliminated, new roles are emerging. Focus on skills that complement AI: creativity, critical thinking, emotional intelligence.

Environmental Impact

Training large AI models requires significant computational resources and energy. A single training run can emit as much carbon as several cars over their lifetime.

Bias & Fairness

AI systems can perpetuate and amplify biases from training data, leading to unfair outcomes for marginalized groups. Be aware of potential bias in outputs.

Transparency & Accountability

Many AI systems are "black boxes" - their decision-making is opaque. Who is responsible when AI makes mistakes or harmful decisions?

Misinformation

AI can generate convincing but false information at scale. Deepfakes and synthetic media can deceive. Always verify important information from multiple reliable sources.

Human-AI Relationship

AI should augment human capability, not replace human judgment in critical decisions. Maintain appropriate boundaries in human-AI interaction.

Best Practices for Responsible AI Use

  • Always Verify AI Outputs: AI generates content confidently even when wrong. Always fact-check critical information before using it.
  • Protect Privacy: Don't share sensitive personal or business data. Assume anything you input may be stored and used for training.
  • Provide Clear Context: AI works better with specific guidance. Give examples, set constraints, and specify what you want.
  • Acknowledge AI Use: Be transparent when content is AI-generated, especially in professional or academic settings.
  • Review for Bias: Check outputs for unfair or discriminatory content. AI reflects biases from its training data.
  • Use AI as a Tool, Not a Decision-Maker: Maintain human oversight and judgment for important decisions. AI assists, humans decide.
  • Understand Limitations: Knowing what AI can't do is as important as knowing what it can. It hallucinates, struggles with logic, and has knowledge cutoffs.
  • Stay Informed: AI capabilities and regulations evolve rapidly. What's impossible today may be standard tomorrow.
